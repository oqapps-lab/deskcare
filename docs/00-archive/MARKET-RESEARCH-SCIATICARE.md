## 6. Key Pain Points for Sciatica Sufferers (App-Based Management)

### Top 5 Pain Points

**1. Generic, One-Size-Fits-All Programs**
Sciatica has multiple causes (herniated disc, piriformis syndrome, spinal stenosis, degenerative disc disease). An exercise that helps one type can worsen another. Existing apps do not differentiate between types or severity levels.

**2. No Context for Office/Sedentary Workers**
Office workers need exercises they can do at their desk, during breaks, or in small spaces. Most apps show floor exercises requiring a mat and changing clothes -- impractical during a workday. The seated figure-4 stretch (most effective for sitting-related sciatica) is rarely highlighted.

**3. Lack of Phase-Appropriate Guidance**
During an acute flare-up (days 1--7), nerve glides and gentle movement are appropriate. During recovery (weeks 2--6), progressive strengthening is key. During maintenance, prevention exercises dominate. No existing consumer app adapts to these phases.

**4. Information Overload Without Curation**
Conflicting advice online (stretch vs. strengthen vs. rest) creates paralysis. Users want a clear, authoritative "do this, not that" protocol. The recommended frequency for office workers is every 60--90 minutes for seated stretches, but no app implements break reminders tied to exercise routines.

**5. Poor Adherence & Motivation Tools**
Non-adherence to home exercises is a well-documented problem in PT. Existing apps lack streak tracking, reminders, progress visualization, pain journaling, and outcome feedback loops that would sustain engagement.

## 9. Regulatory Considerations

### FDA Classification

**SciatiCare would likely qualify as a General Wellness Product (NOT a medical device) under current FDA guidance, provided it follows specific guidelines.**

#### General Wellness Exemption (Updated January 2026)

The FDA's revised guidance clarifies that products intended for maintaining and encouraging a healthy lifestyle and unrelated to the diagnosis, cure, mitigation, prevention, or treatment of a disease or condition are not regulated as medical devices.

**SciatiCare would qualify IF:**
- It promotes general wellness (exercise, flexibility, posture)
- It does NOT claim to diagnose, treat, cure, or prevent sciatica
- It does NOT provide personalized medical recommendations
- It presents low risk to users
- It includes appropriate disclaimers

**SciatiCare would NOT qualify IF:**
- It claims to be a "treatment" for sciatica
- It provides diagnostic functionality (e.g., "you have piriformis syndrome")
- It replaces clinical care or physical therapy
- It uses clinical/therapeutic language in marketing

#### Two-Factor Test for General Wellness

1. **Intended for general wellness use only** -- frame as exercise/stretching for office worker wellness
2. **Low risk to users** -- guided exercise with appropriate safety warnings meets this

### Recommended Legal/Regulatory Approach

| Area | Recommendation |
|------|---------------|
| **Marketing Language** | "Exercises for comfort and flexibility" NOT "treatment for sciatica" |
| **Disclaimers** | "Not a substitute for medical advice. Consult a physician before starting." |
| **Terms of Service** | Strong liability limitation and assumption of risk language |
| **Medical Review** | Have all content reviewed by licensed PTs and physicians |
| **Insurance** | Obtain professional liability insurance |
| **Content Labeling** | "Created with physical therapists" NOT "prescribed by doctors" |
| **International** | CE marking considerations for EU (MDR); each market varies |

### Liability Mitigation

1. **Every exercise should include contraindications** (e.g., "skip this if you have spinal stenosis")
2. **Initial assessment quiz** should screen for red flags and direct users to seek medical attention
3. **In-app warnings** for severe symptoms (numbness, loss of bladder/bowel control)
4. **No auto-generated exercise prescriptions** -- curated by licensed professionals only
5. **Document PT/medical advisory board** prominently
